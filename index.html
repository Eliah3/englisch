Brexit. Causes · Developments · Consequences — a concept map, nine years on.

Scope. The 2016 referendum, the years in between, and the economic & political reality of 2025.
— ※ —
Verdict, in short. Most of the promises did not survive contact with reality.
Did the Brexit the Leave camp promised in 2016 ever actually arrive?
— the spine of this map —
01 / CAUSES
Why Britain voted Leave.
Sovereignty, immigration, elite distrust — and a referendum Cameron never expected to lose.
Political

Sovereignty.

The slogan “Take Back Control” captured a decades-long Euroscepticism. Parliament — not Brussels — should make British laws.

Demographic

Immigration.

Free movement after 2004 EU enlargement was felt unevenly. Priti Patel and UKIP turned migration into the campaign’s emotional core.

Economic

Deindustrial North.

Left-behind regions (Grimsby, the Midlands, Northeast) blamed globalisation and the EU for the loss of fishing, manufacturing and wages.

Tactical

Cameron’s Gamble.

A referendum promised in 2013 to pacify Tory backbenchers and contain UKIP. 72% turnout · 52% for Leave. He resigned the next morning.

02 / DEVELOPMENTS
Nine years, four Prime Ministers.
From vote to deal to aftermath — the process that Dominic Raab said could take ten years.
2016
Referendum: 52 % vote Leave. Cameron resigns. May becomes PM.
2017
Article 50 triggered. General election costs May her majority.
2018
First no-confidence vote against Theresa May in December.
2019
May resigns “with no ill will”. Johnson wins 80-seat landslide on “Get Brexit Done.”
2020
UK formally leaves the EU on 31 Jan. 1,246-page Trade & Cooperation Agreement signed on Boxing Day.
2021
Transition period ends. Customs controls, paperwork, Schengen 90-day rule begin to bite.
2023
Sunak’s Windsor Framework — “green” and “red” lanes for Northern Ireland goods.
2025
Five years since the deal. 61 % of Britons call Brexit a failure. Starmer promises TCA renegotiation.
03 / THE TEN PROMISES
Promise vs. reality.
What the Leave campaign said in 2016 — and what actually happened by 2025.
01
EconomyOsborne (Remain): expect a recession
No immediate crash — but Brexit has caused a 6 % hit to UK GDP (CER doppelganger study), up to 6–8 % by NBER. £140 bn in lost output, £3,400 per Londoner.
Partly true
— worse long-term
02
NegotiationsRaab: 5–10 years to settle a trade deal
The TCA was signed in December 2020 — but disputes on fish, Northern Ireland and financial services dragged on for years. His worst-case estimate was optimistic about scope, not speed.
Half-fulfilled
03
EU Trade RelationshipCameron: return to bureaucracy
Exactly what happened. Two-thirds of UK exporters say trading with the EU is harder. £330 m in new food-import checks. A bottle of French wine costs ~£3.50 more.
Broken
— remainers were right
04
FisheriesLeave: take back control of our waters
Grimsby fishmongers say they wish Brexit had never happened. Icelandic fish now bypasses the UK entirely and goes straight to Boulogne. Red tape > reclaimed waters.
Broken
05
SecurityMay (Remain): safer inside the EU
UK lost automatic access to tools like SIS II and the European Arrest Warrant. Cooperation continues, but more slowly and more expensively. May’s warning held up.
Partly confirmed
06
Northern IrelandJohnson: the border will be “absolutely unchanged”
Instead, a de facto border in the Irish Sea. Protocol → Windsor Framework → DUP boycott of Stormont → risk of “trivergence”. One of the deepest Brexit wounds.
Broken
07
RegulationRaab: cut unnecessary red tape
Bonfire of EU laws was quietly dropped. Paperwork has grown, not shrunk — new customs forms, SPS checks, product certifications. Deregulation turned into re-regulation.
Broken
08
ImmigrationPatel: take back control of our borders
Net migration 2022: 745,000 — more than triple the 2019 Tory target. EU citizens refused entry rose five-fold, but total numbers hit record highs. “Control” ≠ lower.
Broken
— opposite outcome
09
Global Trade DealsLeave: the world will queue up
Australia deal = +0.08 % GDP. India stalled. US shelved. Canada collapsed. ~70 agreements signed but mostly rollovers of existing EU terms.
Broken
10
The UnionGove: the UK will be stronger
Scotland voted Remain by 62 % and has no Brexit mandate. Northern Ireland politically deadlocked. The union is arguably weaker, not stronger.
Broken

06 / OUTLOOK
What happens next?
Rejoin is off the table — but closer alignment is quietly gaining ground.
Public Opinion

A shifting mood.

YouGov: 51 % of Britons now favour rejoining the EU; 42 % would back the Single Market. Only 31 % want the status quo.

Politics

Starmer’s thin line.

Labour promises a TCA renegotiation in 2025 — but rules out Single Market or full membership. Brexit is “the dog that won’t bark.”

Economy

Piecemeal repairs.

Historian David Reynolds: the EU is still the UK’s biggest trading partner, so Britain will have to keep patching the relationship, not rebuild it.
