Brexit. Causes · Developments · Consequences — a concept map, nine years on.

Scope. The 2016 vote, what happened after, and where the UK stands in 2025.
— ※ —
Short version. Most of what Leave promised never happened.
Did Leave actually deliver what it promised in 2016?
— the question this map tries to answer —
01 / CAUSES
Why Britain voted Leave.
A mix of old Euroscepticism, anger over immigration, and a referendum Cameron didn’t think he’d lose.
Political

Sovereignty.

“Take Back Control” was the slogan that stuck. The idea: Parliament should make British laws, not Brussels. Euroscepticism in the UK goes back decades — Leave just gave it a clean sentence.

Demographic

Immigration.

Free movement after the 2004 EU enlargement hit some regions harder than others. Priti Patel and UKIP made migration the issue the Leave campaign ran on.

Economic

Left-behind regions.

Places like Grimsby, the Midlands and the Northeast had been losing fishing and manufacturing jobs for years. A lot of voters blamed the EU and globalisation, not always separately.

Tactical

Cameron’s gamble.

He promised the referendum in 2013 to calm his own backbenchers and stop UKIP. It backfired: 72% turnout, 52% for Leave. He resigned the morning after.

02 / DEVELOPMENTS
Nine years, four Prime Ministers.
From the vote to the deal to the aftermath. Dominic Raab said it might take ten years — he wasn’t completely wrong.
2016
Referendum: 52% vote Leave. Cameron resigns. May becomes PM.
2017
Article 50 triggered. May calls an early election and loses her majority.
2018
First no-confidence vote against May in December.
2019
May resigns. Johnson wins a large majority on the line “Get Brexit Done.”
2020
UK formally leaves the EU on 31 January. The 1,246-page Trade & Cooperation Agreement is signed on Boxing Day.
2021
Transition ends. Customs checks, paperwork and the Schengen 90-day rule kick in.
2023
Sunak signs the Windsor Framework — “green” and “red” lanes for goods going into Northern Ireland.
2025
Five years after the deal, 61% of Britons call Brexit a failure. Starmer says he’ll renegotiate the TCA.
03 / THE TEN PROMISES
Promise vs. reality.
What Leave said in 2016 — and how it actually played out by 2025.
01
EconomyOsborne (Remain): expect a recession
There was no sudden crash, which Brexiteers used as proof they were right. But the long-run damage is real: the CER estimates a 6% hit to UK GDP compared to a non-Brexit scenario, and an NBER paper puts it at 6–8% by 2025. That’s roughly £140bn in lost output and £3,400 per Londoner.
Partly true
— worse long-term
02
NegotiationsRaab: 5–10 years to settle a trade deal
The main deal (TCA) was signed at the end of 2020, so the headline timeline was faster. But fights over fishing rights, Northern Ireland and financial services dragged on for years after that. His estimate was closer to reality than it looked at the time.
Half-fulfilled
03
EU Trade RelationshipCameron: return to bureaucracy
Cameron was basically right. Two-thirds of UK exporters say trading with the EU has got harder. New food-import checks cost £330m. A bottle of French wine is about £3.50 more expensive than before.
Broken
— remainers were right
04
FisheriesLeave: take back control of our waters
Grimsby fishmongers say they wish Brexit had never happened. Icelandic fish now skips the UK entirely and gets shipped straight to Boulogne, because the paperwork makes the old route not worth it.
Broken
05
SecurityMay (Remain): safer inside the EU
The UK lost automatic access to tools like SIS II and the European Arrest Warrant. Cooperation still happens, but it’s slower and more expensive. May’s warning held up pretty well.
Partly confirmed
06
Northern IrelandJohnson: the border will be “absolutely unchanged”
The opposite happened. There’s now effectively a border in the Irish Sea. The Protocol, then the Windsor Framework, then the DUP walking out of Stormont — Northern Ireland is probably the clearest case of Leave getting things wrong.
Broken
07
RegulationRaab: cut unnecessary red tape
The promised “bonfire of EU laws” was quietly dropped. Paperwork went up, not down — new customs forms, SPS checks, product certifications. Businesses are doing more admin now, not less.
Broken
08
ImmigrationPatel: take back control of our borders
Net migration in 2022 hit 745,000 — more than three times what the Tory manifesto promised in 2019. Fewer EU citizens, yes (refusals at the border went up fivefold), but overall numbers are at record highs. “Control” ended up meaning something different than most Leave voters expected.
Broken
— opposite outcome
09
Global Trade DealsLeave: the world will queue up
The Australia deal adds about 0.08% to GDP. India is still stuck, the US talks are paused, Canada collapsed. The UK has signed around 70 agreements, but most are just rollovers of deals it already had through the EU.
Broken
10
The UnionGove: the UK will be stronger
Scotland voted Remain by 62% and has no Brexit mandate. Northern Ireland has spent years politically stuck. If anything, the union looks weaker now than it did in 2016.
Broken

06 / OUTLOOK
What happens next?
Rejoining isn’t on the cards, but attitudes are shifting.
Public Opinion

The mood is changing.

YouGov: 51% of Britons would now rejoin the EU, 42% would go back to the Single Market. Only 31% want to keep things as they are.

Politics

Starmer’s balancing act.

Labour has promised to renegotiate the TCA in 2025, but ruled out rejoining or going back into the Single Market. Brexit is, as TLDR put it, “the dog that won’t bark.”

Economy

Fixing it piece by piece.

The EU is still the UK’s biggest trading partner. Historian David Reynolds argues the only realistic path is patching the relationship, not rebuilding it from scratch.
